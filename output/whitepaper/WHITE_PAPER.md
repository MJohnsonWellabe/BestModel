# Decoding the AM Best Rating: Where Wellabe Stands and What Moves It

**Office of the Chief Actuary · Wellabe · Draft**
*Rating unit: American Enterprise Group (AMB #070369), rated **A (Excellent), Stable**.
Built from Wellabe's Best's Credit Reports (2023–2026), the 2026 strategic plan, and a
50-carrier peer model reconstructed from public AM Best assessments + S&P/SNL statutory data.*

---

## 1. The thesis in one page

AM Best does not score a single number; it assigns four **block assessments** and runs them
through a published **baseline-plus-notching** table to a letter rating. Wellabe's blocks today are
**Strongest** balance sheet, **Adequate** operating performance, **Neutral** business profile,
**Appropriate** ERM — which map to **A**.

Three facts frame everything:

1. **The balance sheet is far safer than a raw RBC reading suggests.** Best rates capital on **BCAR**,
   not NAIC RBC. Wellabe's BCAR at the 99.6% VaR was **67.3%** at year-end 2025 — versus a **25%**
   bar for *Strongest*. That is ~2.7× the threshold. Across four annual reports it has moved only
   73.4 → 73.0 → 71.2 → 67.3, despite three years of new-business strain. Best's published outlook
   text **explicitly expects Strongest to be maintained**.
2. **Operating performance is the real, binding risk** — and it is a *credibility* risk, not a
   capital one. Wellabe sits at the bottom of *Adequate* only because Best credits the growth story:
   "results have declined materially due to new business strain… expected to improve as this business
   matures." That credit is conditional on actuals tracking the plan.
3. **The realistic downside for the medium term is A-, not B++.** A one-notch slip (either block) lands
   A-; the B++ scenario requires *both* blocks to fall, and the balance-sheet leg is unlikely given the
   BCAR cushion and Best's stated expectation. A downgrade is normally pre-signalled by an outlook
   change to Negative, and Best has signalled nothing.

The rest of this paper grounds each of these in the peer model and names the carriers that prove the
mechanics.

## 2. How the rating is actually built (and how we reverse-engineered it)

Best maps the **balance-sheet assessment to a baseline ICR**, then **notches** for operating
performance (+2/−3), business profile (+2/−2) and ERM (+1/−4), then translates the ICR to the FSR
letter. We rebuilt this from 50 carriers' public assessments and found two things that matter for
reading Wellabe's position:

- **The baseline and each notch are *ranges*, not points.** Empirically, half of *Very Strong*
  carriers imply an `a` baseline and half `a-`; the committee places within the band on relative
  strength. So the honest output is a **one-notch band**, e.g. Wellabe today = **A (band A/A-)**.
  This is why two carriers with identical block labels can sit a notch apart — *Pekin* and another
  Very Strong carrier are both "Very Strong/Marginal/Neutral" yet land A- and B++ respectively.
- **Standalone carriers reconcile cleanly; group-supported ones don't.** 22 of 24 standalone carriers
  land inside their predicted band. The misses are subsidiaries carrying a parent's rating (Humana,
  Cigna/HCSC, Lumico/RGA) — irrelevant to Wellabe, which is rated on its own merits.

*Tool reference: the interactive decoder reproduces this for every carrier and includes a Wellabe
calculator that shows the FSR band for any combination of blocks.*

## 3. Balance sheet — secure, and more so than RBC implies

**The peer-RBC thresholds (our proxy).** Sorting the 50 carriers by balance-sheet tier gives clean
lower-quartile floors — and they line up almost exactly with the internal appetite:

| Tier | Peer RBC floor (p25) | Internal read |
|---|---|---|
| Strongest | ~**530%** | "~550% to stay Strongest" — **agree** |
| Very Strong | ~**375%** | the A- / a- zone — **agree** |
| Strong | ~**275%** | Strong floor — **agree** |
| Adequate | ~**215%** | below here capital pressures the rating |

Wellabe holds **~648% RBC** — comfortably inside Strongest on the peer proxy.

**But RBC is not what Best uses — BCAR is, and BCAR says the balance sheet is not the constraint.**
At **67.3%** vs a 25% bar, Wellabe could lose more than half its risk-adjusted capital and still
clear *Strongest*. The plan manages surplus down to a **400% CAL RBC** target (a trough of ~$451M at
YE2028 from $649M at YE2025) — which on the peer proxy looks like "Very Strong" territory, yet BCAR
would almost certainly still read *Strongest*. **This divergence is the single most important nuance
in the balance-sheet story: the proxy understates Wellabe's security; do not over-weight a falling
RBC number.**

**Capital *flexibility* is an underused strength.** Best explicitly credits financial flexibility.
Wellabe has **no debt and no surplus notes outstanding** (full room to issue), FHLB Des Moines
capacity, live structured- and traditional-reinsurance options on Med Supp C-2, and a Final Expense
block in a six-bid sale process. Most of these *add* capital headroom without diluting the rating.

**Named proof.** *Atlantic American — American Southern* is **Strong / A-** at just **213% RBC** — i.e.,
a carrier can hold A- on far less capital than Wellabe when the other blocks hold. The balance sheet
is the *least* of Wellabe's rating risks.

**Watch items (honest):** the institutional reinsurance blocks (ALSC MYGA 100% shock-lapse in 2026,
LSW IUL, AHL hospital-indemnity) bring modco/funds-withheld and counterparty exposure; FHLB draws
($25M/yr through 2033) add leverage. None threatens Strongest today, but they are the things that
would show up first if the balance sheet ever softened.

## 4. Operating performance — the binding risk, and it is about belief

**The data.** Across the sample, operating performance tracks **ROE level *and* stability**: median
5-yr ROE steps Strong **11%** › Adequate **3%** › Marginal **−1.5%**, and the *Marginal* carriers
also carry the highest benefit ratios (~68%). Wellabe runs **negative** recent ROE — three straight
loss years — yet is held at **Adequate**. It is, unambiguously, **at the bottom of Adequate**.
**Agree with the internal read: this is the most likely place a downgrade starts.**

**Why Wellabe is Adequate where look-alikes are Marginal.** This is the crux, and it is *credibility*,
not arithmetic. *Lumico* (−7.5% ROE), *American-Amicable* (−5.2%) and *GPM* (−4.1%) all run similar
or better earnings and are assessed **Marginal**. Wellabe is held a tier higher because Best reads
its losses as **credited new-business strain in support of a growth plan it currently believes** —
the report says so verbatim. Two carriers, identical earnings, different assessment, entirely on
whether Best believes the turn is coming. That belief is Wellabe's most valuable and most fragile
rating asset.

**The cautionary tale — name it.** *Government Personnel Mutual* is **Very Strong** balance sheet
(477% RBC, plenty of capital) yet **B++**, because its operating performance is **Marginal**. That is
*exactly* Wellabe's double-drop scenario made real: capital does not rescue a Marginal operating
assessment. GPM is the carrier to point at.

**What Best is actually watching** (from the plan + report): is performance tracking the projected
turn? The concrete milestones are Med Supp statutory earnings flipping **positive in 2029**, surplus
troughing **2028**, the **2024-charter economics** (Med Supp IRR up to 12.5% from 9.2%), **rerates**
(13.4% achieved in 2025), the underwritten mix, and persistency on the back book. **If actuals miss
the plan materially — or Best simply stops believing it — the Adequate credit converts to Marginal.**

**Named one-notch examples.** *Pekin* (Very Strong/Marginal → **A-**) and *Funeral Directors*
(Very Strong/Adequate/Limited → **A-**) show that a single soft block from a strong base still holds
A-. The drop from Adequate is survivable; it is the *combination* that bites.

## 5. Business profile — Neutral, and it is breadth (not scale) that earns it

The cross-sectional driver of business profile is **scale**: Favorable carriers are ~$30B+, Neutral
specialists ~$1–2B. But **for Wellabe specifically, scale is *not* the differentiator** — and this
corrects a tempting but wrong reading. Wellabe (~$2.3B) sits in a cluster with three **Limited**
carriers — *Funeral Directors* (~$2.7B), *Assurity* (~$2.9B), *Sentinel* — that are **the same size
or larger.** Scale cannot be why Wellabe is Neutral and they are Limited.

**What actually earns Neutral** (straight from the credit report): **product and geographic breadth on
top of the senior niche.** Best cites Wellabe's 40+ state footprint, diverse distribution, and
diversification *beyond* Med Supp into preneed (Great Western), dental, hospital indemnity, short-term
care and critical illness. The Limited carriers are single-market (FDLIC = preneed only; Assurity =
DI/supplemental). **Wellabe is multi-line and broadly distributed; they are not.** The plan reinforces
this: Med Supp in 26 states, HI 28, Dental 29, STC 24, CI 30; preneed 38 states; 159 FMOs and ~33,000
appointed agents; a DTC channel (Wellabe.com → ARIS) and a digital/underwriting edge.

**Agree with the internal read that this won't change soon** — moving to *Favorable* needs a step-change
in scale or a genuine second risk type beyond senior protection, neither imminent. The risk is *down*,
not up: the franchise rests on Med Supp (92% of Med Supp sales from the top 20 states; in-force
concentrated in a highly competitive line). A regulatory or competitive shock to Med Supp is the
business-profile (and operating) tail risk. **The differentiators that hold Neutral are exactly the
things to protect: breadth, distribution quality, and the non-commoditized features (underwriting,
digital, ancillary pairing) that the internal team correctly identifies.**

## 6. ERM — Appropriate and stable

Best assesses ERM **Appropriate**: a developed program, discernible risk culture, established
appetites/tolerances, board oversight. **Agree it is unlikely to move** in either direction over the
horizon; it is neither a driver nor a near-term risk. The one place ERM *could* matter is execution
risk on the growth bet — but that surfaces through operating performance, not ERM.

## 7. The rating math and the scenarios (validated)

Wellabe today: Strongest / Adequate / Neutral / Appropriate → **A (band A/A-)**. Running the internal
scenarios through the model and naming the analog:

| Scenario | Blocks | Model result | Real analog |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Today** | Strongest / Adequate / Neutral / Appropriate | **A** (A/A-) | — |
| Balance sheet slips one tier | Very Strong / Adequate / Neutral / Appropriate | **A-** | Pekin, FDLIC, National Western |
| Operating slips to Marginal | Strongest / Marginal / Neutral / Appropriate | **A-** | — (capital cushions it) |
| **Both slip** | Very Strong / Marginal / Neutral / Appropriate | **B++** (band A-/B+) | **GPM (exactly this)** |

**Agreement with the internal scenario read: yes, on all three.** One slip → A-. Both → B++, and at
that point it is a committee call inside the band. Two refinements:

- **The B++ leg is less likely than it may feel**, because it requires the *balance-sheet* drop, which
  BCAR (67% vs 25%) and Best's stated expectation make improbable in the medium term. The binding,
  realistic risk is the **operating** slip alone → **A-**. Treat **A- as the floor** for the planning
  horizon, with B++ a tail that needs two things to go wrong at once.
- **Timing.** Strain peaks with the 2028 surplus trough; Med Supp earnings flip positive 2029. So the
  *credibility test window is 2027–2028* — if the projected turn is visibly off-track then, that is
  when an outlook change could come. A 2029 downgrade is plausible only if the turn fails to appear.
  **Agree on the reversibility point:** because the trigger is the operating *trend*, a return to
  positive earnings would restore the notch — the path back from B++ to A- is short and is itself a
  reason Best tolerates the strain.

**The signal you'll get first.** A downgrade is normally preceded by **Stable → Negative outlook**.
Best has signalled nothing. That, plus the BCAR cushion, is why "A- or better for the next couple of
years" is a well-supported base case. **Agree.**

## 8. What to lean into — protecting today's categories *(requested deliverable)*

These are the levers, mapped to the block each one defends, all grounded in the reports/plan:

**To keep *Strongest* balance sheet**
- Lead with **BCAR, not RBC**, in any Best dialogue — the cushion (67% vs 25%) is the headline.
- Emphasize **capital flexibility**: no debt, no surplus notes, FHLB capacity, structured-reinsurance
  C-2 relief, the Final Expense sale. These are credible, near-term sources Best already credits.
- Keep asset risk and FHLB leverage visibly disciplined; pre-explain the institutional/modco blocks.

**To keep *Adequate* operating (the one that matters most)**
- **Make the plan-vs-actual story impossible to disbelieve.** Report the concrete turn metrics every
  cycle: rerate achievement (13.4% in 2025), 2024-charter IRR (12.5%), underwritten mix, persistency,
  the 2029 Med Supp positive-earnings inflection, the 2028 surplus trough and rebound.
- Frame losses as **funded, planned strain with positive strain-adjusted earnings in every year after
  2025** — the deck's strongest single fact for this purpose.
- Show **operating leverage building**: declining benefit ratios via rerates, expense margin falling
  (12.6% → 5.2% Med Supp), straight-through processing 55% → 70%.

**To keep *Neutral* business profile**
- Protect and publicize **breadth**, since that — not scale — is what separates Wellabe from the
  Limited cluster: multi-line (Med Supp + preneed + ancillary health + life), 40+ states, broad FMO/
  agent distribution, the DTC/ARIS channel, and the differentiated, non-commoditized capabilities
  (underwriting, MyEasyMatch/digital, ancillary pairing).
- Continue de-concentrating from pure Med Supp (ancillary health growth, preneed) — every credible
  second line moves the franchise away from "single-market specialist."

## 9. What you may be missing / blind spots *(unbiased)*

1. **You are probably over-weighting the balance-sheet downgrade.** The peer-RBC view makes it look
   close; BCAR says it is not. The realistic medium-term floor is **A-** (operating slip), not B++.
2. **The operating risk is *belief*, not just numbers.** Two carriers (Wellabe vs GPM/Lumico) with the
   same earnings get different assessments. The lever is therefore as much *communication and tracking*
   as it is hitting the plan — Best must be able to see the turn coming.
3. **Business profile is breadth, not size.** Don't argue scale against FDLIC/Assurity (you're not
   bigger). Argue diversification, geography, distribution and product sophistication — that is what
   Best actually cited.
4. **Concentration is the quiet tail risk.** 92% of Med Supp from 20 states and in-force concentrated
   in a highly competitive, rerate-dependent line. A regulatory or competitive shock to Med Supp hits
   operating *and* business profile at once — the one event that could move two blocks together.
5. **The institutional/reinsurance complexity** (ALSC shock-lapse, LSW, AHL, HSA, modco reserves, FHLB)
   is a balance-sheet and ERM watch area that the RBC-only lens misses.
6. **ESG is not a separate rating factor** for a carrier like Wellabe — Best folds it into the existing
   blocks. No independent ESG pressure; don't over-invest analytically there.
7. **The reversibility is an asset — say it out loud to Best.** Because the operating trigger is a
   *trend*, the path from a hypothetical B++ back to A- is one good earnings print. That is part of why
   the strain is tolerated, and it argues for transparency over conservatism in the dialogue.
